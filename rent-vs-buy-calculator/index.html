What the comparison includes

Buying and renting each have costs the other doesn't. A fair comparison tracks the full picture over your time horizon:

  • Buying — down payment, closing costs, mortgage, property tax, insurance, maintenance; offset by equity and appreciation, minus selling costs when you leave.
  • Renting — rent (which grows over time), offset by investing the money you didn't tie up in a down payment at your expected return.

The tool charts the cumulative net cost of each path and marks the crossover year — the point where buying becomes cheaper than renting.

Common questions

Is it better to rent or buy?

Mostly a question of how long you stay. Renting usually wins the first few years; past the crossover (often 5–7 years) buying tends to pull ahead. Your rate, prices, and rent decide the exact point.

What is the opportunity cost of a down payment?

What that cash could earn if invested instead. An $80,000 down payment at 6% is a real cost of buying that most calculators skip — including it keeps the comparison honest.

What is the crossover year?

The year buying's cumulative net cost drops below renting's. Stay past it and buying pays off.
