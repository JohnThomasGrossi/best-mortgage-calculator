How a recast works

You pay a large amount toward principal, and the lender recalculates (re-amortizes) your monthly payment across the original remaining term on the new, smaller balance. You keep your interest rate — valuable when it's lower than today's — and pay only a modest recast fee. The result is a permanently lower required payment without the cost or paperwork of a refinance.

Recast vs. prepay: two ways to use the same cash

  • Recast — lowers the monthly payment, keeps the original payoff date. Best for cash-flow relief.
  • Prepay — apply the lump sum but keep paying the old amount, so the loan ends sooner and saves more interest overall.

Common questions

What is a mortgage recast?

A lump-sum principal payment after which the lender recalculates your payment over the original remaining term. Rate and payoff date stay; the payment drops. Fees are typically $150–$500.

Recast or refinance?

Recast keeps your current rate for a small fee — ideal when your rate is good. Refinance swaps in a new rate/term with full closing costs, better only if rates have dropped significantly.

Recast or just prepay?

Recast lowers the payment; prepaying keeps it and pays off sooner, saving more interest. The calculator shows both side by side.
